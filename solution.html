Solutions for ACEs

How to care for someone who has been through trauma ?

Provide Social Support

  • Don't pressure them into talking about it, let them take the lead
  • Be patient
  • Educate yourself about ACEs
  • Accept(and expect) mixed feelings
  • Be a good listener

Don't

  • Give easy answers or blithely tell them everything is going to be okay
  • Stop them from talking about their feelings or fears
  • Offer unsolicited advice or tell them what they “should” do
  • Invalidate, minimize, or deny one’s traumatic experience
  • Give ultimatums or make threats or demands
  • Make them feel weak because they aren’t coping as well as others
  • Tell them they were lucky it wasn’t worse
  • Take over with your own personal experiences or feelings

Build trust and safety

  • Keep promises
  • Empathize with their strengths

Anticipate and manage triggers

  • Tell them they’re having a flashback and that even though it feels real, the event is not actually happening again
  • Help remind them of their surroundings (for example, ask them to look around the room and describe out loud what they see)
  • Encourage them to take deep, slow breaths (hyperventilating will increase feelings of panic)
  • Avoid sudden movements or anything that might startle them
  • Ask before you touch them. Touching or putting your arms around the person might make them feel trapped, which can lead to greater agitation and even violence

Deal with volatility and anger

  • Watch for signs they are angry
  • Try to remain calm
  • Give them space
  • Ask how you can help
  • Help them to manage their anger

Positive Childhood Experiences- Counter ACEs

According to various researches, positive experiences can counter the effects of adverse experiences. The positive experiences that the study measured included:

  • Being able to talk to family about feelings
  • Having a family stand by them during difficult times
  • Participating in community traditions
  • Feeling a sense of belonging in high school
  • Being supported by friends
  • Having non-parents adults take an interest in them
  • Feeling safe and protected by an adult at home

A major takeaway is that even when we cannot prevent adverse experiences, we do have the ability to help counteract their effects.
